What's my pick-of-the-week or tip-of-the-week?

My pick of the week this week is a fairly simple randomized algorithm for verifying polynomial equality. Let's say you have a polynomial f(x) expressed as the product of multiple polynomials and another polynomial g(x), and you want to know if f(x) = g(x). Now, you're probably wondering, why don't you just multiply all the smaller polynomials that compose f(x) and compare that to g(x)? That'll give you the right answer 100% of the time. That's true, but that can be difficult to code and, depending on the way you code it, quite computationally complex. A simple solution is this: given that the degree of f(x) and g(x) is d, choose a number r from the set {1, 2, ..., 100d} uniformly at random (that is, r has an equal probability of being any number from that set). Then, compute the values f(r) and g(r). If f(r) = g(r), then output that f(x) = g(x). If f(r) != g(r), then output that f(x) != g(x). This algorithm can only give the wrong output if f(r) = g(r) but f(x) != g(x), which happens with probability at most 1/100. Why? If f(r) = g(r), that means the polynomial f(x) - g(x) has root r. By the fundamental theorem of algebra, a polynomial can have at most as many roots as the value of its degree. Since f(x) - g(x) has degree d, it can have at most d roots. That means the probability of choosing r as a root of f(x) - g(x) is at most d/100d, or 1/100.
